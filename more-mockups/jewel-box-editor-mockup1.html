Chapter Nine

Low Tide

The water went out before dawn and took the boats with it, leaving them canted in the mud like things that had been dropped and forgotten. Mara watched from the sea wall while the gulls argued over whatever the tide had abandoned, and she thought, not for the first time, that the estuary kept better accounts than her father ever had.

He had gone down to the weir at first light, the way he always did, as if the fish might have changed their habits overnight. She let him go without a word, which was its own kind of cruelty, and the silence followed him out across the flats until the fog took the shape of him entirely.

By the time the sun cleared the headland, the channel had filled again, brown and unhurried, and the town turned its back on the water as it did every day, pretending the sea was a neighbour and not a landlord.
